TLDR View

Abilities (Stats)

Each creature has six abilities to describe their physical and mental characteristics.

The three main rolls of the game--the ability check, the saving throw, and the attack roll--rely on the six ability scores of your character's stats.

Strength (STR):- Measures bodily power, athletic ability, and extent to which you can exert raw physical force

Dexterity (DEX):- Measures agility, reflexes, and balance.

Constitution (CON):- Measures health, stamina, and vital force.

Intelligence (INT):- Measures mental acuity, accuracy of recall, and the ability to reason.

Wisdom (WIS):- Measures how attuned you are to the world around you and represents perceptiveness and intuition

Charisma (CHA):- Measures your ability to interact effectively with others.

Ability Scores and Modifiers

Each ability has a score, a number that defines the magnitue of that ability. A score of 10 or 11 is the normal human average, but adventurers and many monsters are cut above average in most abilities. A score of 18 is the highest that a person usually reaches, but some creatures are known to have a score as high as 20.

Each ability also has a modifier, derived from the score and ranging from -5 to +10. To determine an ability modifier subtract 10 from the ability score and then divide the total by 2 (rounding down).

Ability modifiers affect many aspects of the game. The are used in attack rolls, ability checks, and saving throws. Ability modifiers come up in play more often than their associated scores.

Proficiency Bonus

Characters have a proficiency bonus determined by level. You add this bonus to ability checks, saving throws, and attack rolls.

When making a saving throw, ability check, or an attack roll that your character is proficient in, you may add your proficiency bonus to that roll.

Ability Checks

An ability check tests a character's innate talent and training in an effort to overcome a challenge. The DM decides which of the six abilities is relevant to the task at hand.

To make an ability check, roll a d20 and add the relevant ability modifier. If the total is higher or equal to the required check it is successful; otherwise, it's a failure.

Example: If a character attempts to lift a large object, they must make a strength check to see if they are able to.

Skills

A skill represents a specific aspect of an ability score, and an individual's proficiency in a skill demonstrates a focus on that aspect.

Strength: Athletics

Dexterity: Acrobatics, Sleight of Hand, Stealth

Intelligence: Arcana, History, Investigation, Nature, Religion

Wisdom: Animal Handling, Insight, Medicine, Perception, Survival

Charisma: Deception, Intimidation, Performance, Persuasion

Saving Throws

A saving throw, or save, represents an attempt to resist a spell, trap, poison, disease, or similar threat.

Each class gives proficiency in at least two saving throws. To make a saving throw, roll a d20 and add the appropriate ability modifier. If you have proficiency in the ability, you also add your proficiency modifier to the total.

Example: If your character attempts to dodge a falling tree, you must make a dex saving throw to see if you dodge or not.

Advantage and Disadvantage

In some situations your character may an advantage or a disadvantage on an ability check.

When you have an advantage or disadvantage on a roll, you roll a second D20. If you have an advantage, you take the higher of the two rolls. If you have a disadvantage, you take the lower of the two rolls.
